Trebuchet Simulator

Counterweight launch explorer — 0.5–1.5 kg projectile, 20–25 m target band

This is an educational physics simulator for a counterweight trebuchet. Adjust the geometry, counterweight, and losses, then watch the throw and the projectile flight computed by numerical integration. The model is a deliberate simplification: real-world results depend on friction, structural flexibility, release-pin mechanics, sling behaviour, aerodynamics, material strength, and construction quality that this model only approximates. Do not use it as a structural-engineering or construction tool.

Physics assumptions & equations

Everything below is an approximation chosen for transparency, implemented with SI units throughout. The same equations appear as comments in simulation.js.

1. Throw phase — quasi-static energy balance

The arm is treated as a rigid body rotating about a frictionless pin at the pivot (user-set friction is applied as an energy-loss percentage instead of a torque). For each arm angle θ between the cocked angle and the release angle, the model balances energy:

Mcw·g·Δhcw(θ)·η = ½·Itotal·ω²(θ) + mp·g·Δhp(θ) + marm·g·Δharm(θ)

where η = counterweight efficiency × (1 − pivot friction loss) × (1 − structural-flex loss). Solving for ω(θ) gives the arm’s angular speed at every angle; integrating dt = dθ/ω produces the throw-phase timing used by the animation. The counterweight’s potential energy is never assigned entirely to the projectile — the arm, the counterweight’s own residual motion, and the projectile share it through the combined inertia.

2. Rotational inertia

  • Arm: a uniform beam of total length L1+L2. Its centre of mass therefore sits at the beam midpoint, an offset d = (L1−L2)/2 from the pivot toward the throwing end; the inertia about the pivot follows from the parallel-axis theorem: Iarm = (1/12)·marm·(L1+L2)² + marm·d²
  • Fixed counterweight: a point mass at the short-arm tip: Icw = Mcw·L2²
  • Hinged counterweight: a hanging weight falls more vertically and retains less rotational energy, so its coupling is scaled by k = 0.5: Icw = 0.5·Mcw·L2². This is a crude stand-in for real pendulum dynamics, which would require a multi-body simulation.
  • Projectile + sling: the sling is assumed taut and aligned with the arm at release, so the projectile orbits at radius rp = L1 + Ls: Ip = mp·rp²

3. Sling and release

The sling model is kinematic, not dynamic: its angle relative to the arm is interpolated from the initial fold-back angle to fully aligned at release (a real sling whips according to its own equation of motion). Launch speed is v = ωrel·rp·(1 − sling loss), directed perpendicular to the arm-plus-sling line. Choosing the release angle sets the arm position at which the pin lets go, which in this geometry equals the launch elevation angle.

4. Flight phase — numerical integration

After release the projectile is integrated with the semi-implicit Euler method at the user-set time step. In drag mode the aerodynamic force is quadratic:

Fdrag = ½·ρ·Cd·A·|vrel|²,   vrel = v − vwind

with horizontal wind included in the relative velocity. Integration stops when the projectile crosses the landing plane (ground, or the optional target height), and the exact impact point is interpolated between the last two steps. The vacuum curve shown for comparison uses the same integrator with drag switched off; the closed-form range formula is used only as a cross-check in the self-test panel, never as the primary result.

5. What the model ignores

  • True multi-body dynamics of arm + hinged counterweight + sling (replaced by the energy balance and the fixed hinge coupling factor)
  • Sling tension, whip dynamics, and release-pin geometry (replaced by an interpolated sling angle and a user-set release angle and loss percentage)
  • Frame compliance, wheel/base motion, axle friction as torque, air resistance on the arm and sling
  • Projectile spin (Magnus effect), vertical wind, gusts, and altitude-dependent air density
  • Material strength — the simulator will happily "launch" configurations that would break a real machine
